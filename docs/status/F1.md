wrong command's flags anyway.

What the attempt PROVED works, against a real vendor rather than a fixture:
- v2 `PAYMENT-REQUIRED` header detected and decoded.
- Two accepts offered: `exact` (index 0) and `aggr_deferred` (index 1). The

which removes the choice entirely. The signature covers the payment fields, not
the accepts array, so the vendor accepts the narrowed-payload header identically.

build against is the shape a live vendor actually sends.

## 2026-07-21 — real and simulated spend are now separate ledgers

Found while dry-running G2: the run failed with `CAP_EXCEEDED` even though only
15 base units of real money had ever been spent. Cause: **simulated and real
payments shared one spend ledger.** `spent_today` read 1,200,015 — 15 units of
actual money plus 1.2M of eval and demo traffic.

Two ways that bites, and the first one actually happened:
1. A day of tests exhausts the real daily cap and blocks a real payment.
2. `/caps` — the operator's view of money — reports simulated spend as real.

Fix: `procurer_calls.mode` ('real' | 'simulated'). Cap aggregation filters by
mode, so each ledger has its own budget and tests still prove cap enforcement
against their own. `/caps` reports `spent_today` (real only) and
`simulated_today` separately. Existing rows were backfilled from the recorded
receipt rather than guessed — a `SIMULATED:` tx was never real money. Result on
this machine: 20 simulated rows, 1 real row, correctly classified.

An idempotency key that already holds a record in one mode now REFUSES a request
in the other, rather than replaying across. Returning a settled simulated receipt
to a real caller would look exactly like a completed payment that never happened.

Four tests pin it. Verified: 64 procurer, 27 pytest, 28 gateway, 10 evals.

**Latent footgun found, NOT fixed, deliberately.** `reserveCall` takes both a
`ceiling` object (persisted as the reservation) and a `ceilingUnits` number (used
for cap math). Production always derives both from `max_amount` so they agree,
but nothing enforces it — and in the test fixtures they diverge, which is what
made my first ledger test silently record 0. Deriving units from the ceiling
inside `reserveCall` would remove the whole class of bug, but it changes the
signature and several existing tests deliberately pass mismatched values, so
rewriting them hours before submission is the riskier move. Recorded for after.

### The first attempt failed, and it mattered

Attempt 1 ended `failed_refunded` AFTER paying. The vendor delivered correctly —
real ETH balance for block 21000000, `code: "0"` — and **our validator rejected
it**, so The Firm fired OKLink and refunded.

Cause: `validate()` asserted the key names our own mock fixtures emit
(`observations`/`checklist`/`sections`, plus a mandatory `generated_at`). No real
marketplace vendor uses those. **Every real vendor would have failed validation,
been fired, and had the job refunded — after being paid.**

Three reasons this was worse than an economic bug:
1. It inverts the product. The Darwinian layer is meant to manufacture vendor
   intelligence; instead it manufactured false accusations.
2. It corrupted real data. `vendor_performance` recorded agent 2023 at
   adjustment −30, the floor, against a vendor with 1,572 completed sales.
3. The provenance receipt recorded the accusation in writing
   (`"reason": "validation failed: schema, freshness, semantic_sanity"`).
   Showing that on camera would be presenting a fabricated vendor failure —
   exactly what integrity rule 1 forbids.

Cleanup done before anything else: deleted agent 2023's performance row (every
entry in it was an artefact of our own bugs — 3 "timeouts" were our
CAP_EXCEEDED rejections where the vendor was never called, the "success" was a
simulated run, the "validation failure" was a correct response), and dropped the
fabricated provenance. **The payment rows were deliberately left untouched** —
that is real money and must stay auditable.

### The fix (Poulav chose shape-agnostic)

The validator no longer asserts anyone's success schema, because we cannot know
an arbitrary vendor's. It now checks what is knowable:
- the vendor did not report its own error (`error`/`errors` keys, or a status
  field like `code`/`status`/`success` carrying a non-success value),
- the response carries actual content, with status/message keys excluded so a
  bare `code: "0"` cannot pass on the strength of its own status code,
- freshness **only where a timestamp exists** — which is what INTERFACES §6
  specified all along; the old mandatory check contradicted the spec,
- the deterministic semantic floor, unchanged.

Our own fixture shape still passes unchanged. Nine tests pin it, using the real
OKLink payload as the fixture.

### Idempotency proven again, under a real retry

The re-run after the fix did NOT pay twice: `spent_today` went 15 → 30 across
**three** worker runs and two payments. The second run returned the recorded
receipt, which is why the final provenance carries the tx from the original
call. This is the guarantee holding under a genuine retry rather than a
contrived test.

Verified: 35 pytest, 64 procurer, 28 gateway, 10 evals.

## 2026-07-21 — closed the reserveCall footgun (deferred this morning, safe now)

I deferred this earlier as "the riskier move hours before submission". That
judgement was based on an assumption I had not checked: that existing tests
relied on passing a ceiling and a units value that disagreed. They do not —
**every** existing call site already passes matching values. Checking that turned
a risky refactor into a mechanical one, so it is now done.

The bug class: `reserveCall(request, ceilingUnits, caps, mode)` did cap
arithmetic on the number and persisted the object, with nothing enforcing that
the two described the same amount. Production always derived both from
`max_amount`, so they agreed — but a caller passing a ceiling of 0 with units of
50,000 would clear every cap check and then reserve nothing, silently
under-claiming budget on money code. That is not hypothetical: it is exactly
what my own ledger test did this morning, and it recorded 0 without complaint.

Now the units are derived from the ceiling inside `reserveCall`, so the two
cannot disagree — the misuse is no longer expressible. `reserveRefund` had the
same shape and got the same treatment.

I also checked the thing that worried me most: whether any cap test was
**vacuous** because it persisted 0 while asserting on accumulation. None were —
every accumulation test sets an explicit ceiling. A vacuous money-safety test
would have been worse than no test, so this was worth confirming rather than
assuming.

Verified beyond the unit tests: a live simulated `/pay-and-call` against a real
procurer process reserves and settles 15 units correctly. 65 procurer, 38
pytest, 28 gateway, 10 evals.

## 2026-07-21 — pre-payment vendor vetting, and a marketplace health scan at real scale

### Why

The README claims The Firm "background-checks vendors before paying". It did
not. `vetting_node` in the worker logs a state transition and returns; the only
thing standing between a bad vendor and our wallet was the cap check inside
`/pay-and-call`, which fires after we have already chosen that vendor and
started the call. That is a real claim-versus-code gap, and it is the kind a
reviewer finds by reading one function.

The other half: the reliability research (D4) produced the one genuinely novel
dataset we have, and it was a throwaway script. Research that cannot be re-run
is an anecdote.

### What landed

`packages/procurer/src/vet.ts` — an unpaid probe that reads a vendor's 402 and
reports what hiring them would actually cost. Seven verdicts: `X402_OK`,
`PRICE_MISMATCH`, `OVER_BUDGET`, `NO_CHARGE`, `UNSUPPORTED_CHALLENGE`,
`HTTP_ERROR`, `UNREACHABLE`. Exposed at `POST /vet` (single or batch) and as
`pnpm -F @firm/procurer vet`.

It shares `parseChallenge`/`selectOffer` with the money path, so the vet reads a
challenge exactly the way the payer would. I deliberately did **not** refactor
`vendor.ts` to share the fetch plumbing: the interpretation is what has to
match, and that already lives in `x402.ts`. Touching the money path for tidiness
three days before submission is not a trade I would take.

Two properties I wanted pinned rather than assumed:

- **Vetting cannot spend.** `vet.ts` never imports the signer and never reads a
  key. There is a structural test asserting that against the source text, because
  this is the one place that talks to vendor endpoints without a cap check in
  front of it, and that is only safe because it cannot sign.
- **Vetting is not an authorisation.** `hireable: true` narrows the candidate
  list; it never widens what may be paid. A vendor can reprice between the probe
  and the call, so `/pay-and-call` still re-reads the challenge and re-checks
  every cap against the amount it is about to sign. Both facts are in the module
  comment, so nobody later "optimises" the second probe away.

### Validation: the product code reproduces the hand research

Ran it against the same 10 agents D4 probed by hand. **9 of 10 verdicts match
exactly.** The tenth is a finding, not a discrepancy — see below.

### The retry, and why it exists

SignalForge #6560 was recorded UNREACHABLE by the hand research. The product
code reached it. Probing directly three times: fail, 402, 402.

It is not dead, it is cold — free hosting spins containers down. A single probe
cannot distinguish "dead" from "cold-starting", and condemning a live vendor on
one timeout is the same class of error as the validator bug that libelled OKLink.
So `vetVendor` retries **network-level failures only**, twice by default, and
reports `attempts` so a flaky vendor stays visibly flaky. A 402 or a 404 is a
definitive answer and gets exactly one shot — retrying those would triple our
traffic against every dead endpoint on the marketplace for no information.
Both directions are tested.

SignalForge also charges **50x its listing** (10,000 listed, 500,000 live). So
the mispricing count went from one to two before the scan even widened.

### Marketplace health scan — 95 agents

`data/marketplace-health-2026-07-21.json`, regenerable with:

    pnpm -F @firm/procurer vet -- --index data/marketplace-scan.json --out <path>

Population: of 218 scanned agents, **95 publish an A2MCP service with an HTTP
endpoint at all**. The other 123 are A2A-only or serviceless — nothing to probe.
One unpaid POST to the first endpoint-bearing service per agent.

- **41 of 95 (43%) dead or misrouted** at their listed endpoint
- 54 hireable
- **5 charge above their listing**: Clawby 600x, SignalForge 50x, Alpha Radar 5x,
  MarketBrew 3.3x, X API MCP 1.8x
- **7 serve for free despite listing a price** — mispricing in the other
  direction, and presumably nobody has told them

Cost: zero. Nothing was signed. 95 probes in ~7s.

**Methodology caveat, recorded in the report itself:** this probes the *first*
endpoint-bearing service per agent, not all 395. An agent whose first service is
dead may have live ones. The honest claim is "43% of endpoint-bearing agents have
a dead first endpoint", not "43% of services are dead". Do not let this get
rounded up in a thread.

### Availability is not stable within an hour

Predict Protocol #2134 answered a conformant 402 at 19:13 and was consistently
unreachable across five probes from ~19:20 — its host stopped resolving mid-session.
Three consecutive full index runs after that were stable 15/15, so this is not
probe noise.

I think this is the strongest evidence we have for the product's own thesis, and
it arrived by accident. The case against The Firm is "thin wrapper, the buyer
could have called the vendor directly". The measured answer is that a buyer doing
that faces a 43% dead rate, prices that lie by up to 600x, and vendors that stop
answering between the quote and the call. Someone has to re-check, re-hire, and
carry that risk. That is the whole product, and it is now measured rather than
asserted.

### Not done, deliberately

`vetting_node` is still a no-op. Wiring `/vet` into it is ~30 lines in
`apps/firm`, which is F3's lane, and this is an enhancement rather than a G2
blocker — so it is Ishita's call, not a second unilateral lane crossing. The
endpoint, the CLI and the exact call shape are ready for her. Until she wires it,
the background-check claim should be made about the CLI and `/vet`, which are
real and demoable, and **not** about the Projects flow.

Known rough edge: a malformed `/vet` body returns `PROCURER_ERROR` (500) rather
than a 400. `/pay-and-call` behaves the same way, so I left it consistent rather
than fixing one route.

## 2026-07-21 — the stack is deployable, and running it found what reading it did not

Codex C1: "public deployment scaffold is unreachable and incomplete". Both
halves were true, and the second half was worse than the review knew.

### Unreachable

Both servers hard-coded `server.listen(port, "127.0.0.1")`. In a container that
is not a small mistake: `-p 8790:8790` publishes the host port to the
container's *external* interface, and a process bound to the container's
loopback answers nothing on it. The endpoint would have been dead on arrival —
which is verbatim what OKX told Treasury: "unable to reach your Agent's service
endpoint".

Both now take `HOST`, defaulting to loopback so laptops are unchanged, and the
Dockerfiles set `0.0.0.0`.

Two refusals came with it, because both configurations are money bugs wearing
config-mistake costumes:

- The **gateway refuses to bind a public interface in `bypass` mode.** A public
  bypassed gateway does unlimited paid work for free while still paying vendors
  from our wallet. `ALLOW_PUBLIC_BYPASS=true` for deliberate staging.
- The **procurer refuses a non-loopback bind without `PROCURER_AUTH_TOKEN`**
  (Codex C2). It is a spending API with no other gate: whatever can POST
  `/pay-and-call` can spend up to the caps. Bearer token, constant-time compare,
  `/health` and `/caps` left open for container healthchecks. No token
  configured means no check, so nothing about local development changes.

Both refusals verified by actually starting the processes and watching them
exit.

### Incomplete

The image deployed the gateway and nothing else. The gateway takes payment and
creates a job; the *worker* turns a job into a deliverable. Deploy the gateway

indistinguishable from an agent that took your money and vanished.

`docker-compose.deploy.yml` now runs the real stack: db, procurer, worker,

## 2026-07-21 — asset and network authorization, and why I did not take the review's advice literally

Codex C1: "live asset/network/scale authorization is not closed". The allow-list
was optional, there was no network allow-list at all, and an offer that declared
no decimal scale proceeded on the caller's assumption.

The first two are straightforwardly right.

**Network allow-list.** An asset allow-list without a network allow-list is
half a check. "15 units of token X" is meaningless without the chain: a
contract at a familiar-looking address on a chain we never meant to touch is a
different asset, and the cap arithmetic cannot tell — base units are integers.
`selectOffer` now filters on both, and it filters *before* choosing the cheapest
entry rather than checking the winner afterwards, so a challenge offering a bad
cheap entry and a good expensive one is still payable on the good one.

**Both lists are now mandatory in real mode.** The procurer refuses to start
with `REAL_PAYMENTS_ENABLED=true` and either list unset. Verified by starting
it: refuses with neither, refuses with assets only, starts with both.

### Where I disagreed, and why

The review's third point was: require a declared decimal scale whenever real
money moves. I implemented that, then checked it against the marketplace scan
before trusting it.

**41 of the 47 live x402 vendors declare no `extra.decimals`** — including
OKLink #2023, the vendor G1 and G2 both actually paid. That rule would have cut
the hireable pool from 47 to 6 and broken the one payment path with real
transactions behind it. It would have looked like a security improvement and
functioned as a product outage.

The check is not really about the vendor declaring the scale. It is about *us
not knowing it*. So the rule became: the scale must be **known**, not
**declared**. An undeclared scale is safe exactly when the asset and network are
both explicitly allow-listed, because then the scale is a fact about a token we
chose in advance rather than a claim the vendor is making. USD₮0 on X Layer is
6 decimals whether or not the seller says so.

Since `selectOffer` has already refused anything outside both lists, reaching
the check with both configured means the asset is one we named ourselves. The
allow-list *is* the decimals assertion.

Four tests pin the corners: refused with no lists, refused with only the asset
list, paid with both — that last one is the OKLink case and it must keep
working — and simulation left permissive so the fixture vendors are unaffected.

Confirmed against the live vendor rather than a fixture: OKLink's challenge
selects at 15 base units, USD₮0, eip155:196, `declaredDecimals: null`, payable
under both lists; and refused outright when the network list says eip155:1.

### Operational consequence, for whoever fires the next live payment

`X402_ALLOWED_NETWORKS=eip155:196` is now required alongside
`X402_ALLOWED_ASSETS`. The G1/G2 command needs it added or the procurer will
not start. Documented in .env.deploy.example and docker-compose.deploy.yml.

Tests: 95 procurer, 36 gateway, 38 pytest.

## 2026-07-21 — two integrity findings in apps/firm, fixed with a lane note

These are Ishita's files. I crossed the boundary because both are violations of
the integrity rules CLAUDE.md calls absolute, both are small, and both were
found by an external review rather than invented by me. Flagging loudly rather
than quietly: **Ishita should review these two commits specifically.**

### The procurement loop fabricated vendor failures (Codex C1)

`_procure_subtask` recorded `record_timeout(vendor)` — a −10 trust penalty — for
**every** non-ok procurer response. That set includes `CAP_EXCEEDED`,
`UNSUPPORTED_CHALLENGE`, `REQUIRES_HUMAN` and `PROCURER_ERROR`, none of which
are anything the vendor did. They are our spending limit, our signer's
limitation, our policy, and our bug respectively.

This is the same failure class as the G2 validator bug that libelled OKLink,
surviving in a different function after that one was fixed.

It is also demo-blocking, which is how I would have found it the hard way. The
Clawby beat — hire a vendor listed at 5,000 whose live 402 demands 3,000,000,
and let the cap refuse it on camera — routes straight through this line. We
would have filmed ourselves recording a fabricated −10 against a live agent for
the crime of us having a spending limit, in the same demo where we claim not to
fabricate vendor failures.

Now split by fault. Firm-side codes produce a `VendorRejection` carrying the
real reason and **no** performance penalty; genuine vendor faults
(`VENDOR_TIMEOUT`, `VENDOR_ERROR`) still penalise, because a Darwinian layer
that never records anything has stopped learning. `PAYMENT_FAILED` is
deliberately treated as not-the-vendor's-fault: it can mean a stale signature of
ours as easily as a misbehaving vendor, and given the history the tie goes to
not accusing anyone.

The receipt improves as a side effect. "Not hired (CAP_EXCEEDED): vendor asked
for 3000000, above max_amount 5000" is both true and a better demo line than
"vendor timed out".

### The receipt claimed retained revenue on refunded jobs (Codex C0)

`build_provenance` computed `margin = quoted price − outlay` regardless of
outcome. On a refunded job the quoted price went back to the buyer, so revenue
is zero and every unit paid to vendors came out of our own pocket — the
guarantee working as advertised, and the best story in the entry.

Instead the receipt reported it inverted. A 600,000 job with 300,000 of vendor
cost read "300,000 **retained**" when the truth was "300,000 **absorbed**". The
sign was wrong on the single number that carries the whole guarantee claim, and
wrong in our favour.

Fixed: revenue is 0 when `guarantee_status == "refunded"`, so margin is the
negative of total outlay and the receipt says "absorbed".

### Tests

Three new, and I ran the negative control rather than trusting them: disabling
the fault split and restoring the old margin formula fails exactly
`test_our_own_cap_is_never_recorded_as_a_vendor_failure` and
`test_a_refunded_job_reports_absorbed_not_retained`, and nothing else. The
"a real vendor timeout is still recorded" test is there so the fix cannot drift
into never penalising anyone.
